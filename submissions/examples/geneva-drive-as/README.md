# Geneva Drive

A pure CSS/HTML Geneva drive: a crank turning at a dead steady rate, and a wheel that answers in exact 60 degree steps.

## What it shows

The driver carries a single pin. The driven wheel has six radial slots. Once per revolution the pin enters a slot, sweeps the wheel round by exactly one sixth of a turn, and leaves. For the remaining two thirds of the revolution the wheel is held motionless by a locking cam. Continuous in, intermittent out.

The whole mechanism rests on one number:

```
r = C sin(pi / n)
```

the crank radius as a fraction of the centre distance. At exactly that radius the pin enters and leaves **along the axis of the slot**, and the consequence is worth stating plainly: the driven wheel's angular velocity is **exactly zero** at both handovers. It starts from rest and arrives at rest. Choose any other crank radius and the pin arrives across the slot instead of along it, snatching the wheel from standstill to full speed in no time at all, which is to say with infinite acceleration. Nothing physical survives that.

This is why the Geneva drive exists and a ratchet will not do. A ratchet also produces intermittent motion, but it bangs. This one hands over smoothly.

It earned its keep in film projectors. Cine film does not stream past the lens; each frame must be yanked into position and then held **perfectly still** while the shutter opens, twenty four times a second. A Geneva movement does exactly that, and the dwell is where the picture actually happens. The name comes from Geneva and Swiss watchmaking, where the Geneva stop limited a mainspring to a set number of turns so it could not be over-wound. The four slot version, whose driven wheel really does look the part, is called a Maltese cross.

One honest limitation: velocity is continuous but **acceleration is not**. It jumps at the moment of engagement, so the jerk is infinite even though the acceleration is finite. High speed machinery uses modified slot profiles for exactly this reason.

## How it is built

The driven wheel's outline is generated by a radial scan: a disc, minus six radial slots, minus six concave scallops that the locking cam sits in. That comes out as a 720 point `clip-path`. The wheel's rotation is baked from `beta(alpha) = atan2(r sin alpha, C - r cos alpha)` during engagement and held flat during dwell. The driver is a plain constant rotation.

- **The design condition holds exactly**: `r / C = 0.500000` against `sin(pi/6) = 0.500000`.
- **The index is exact.** Sweeping the paused animation over 720 samples of a full driver revolution, the wheel advances **60.0000 degrees**, which is `360/n` with nothing left over.
- **The dwell is real.** The wheel is measured completely stationary for **66.5%** of the driver's revolution, against the predicted **66.7%**; the small shortfall is the sampling resolution near the handover, where the motion is real but vanishingly slow.
- **The rendered wheel obeys the slot equation.** Recomputing `beta(alpha)` from the geometry and comparing against the wheel's measured rotation across **236 engaged samples**: worst disagreement **0.006 degrees**.
- **It starts and stops at rest**, which is the point of the whole design: `dbeta/dalpha` at the handover angle evaluates to **-5.6e-10**, zero to within floating point.

No images, no JavaScript, no external assets.

## Accessibility

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` freezes crank, cam and wheel together, mid-engagement or mid-dwell depending where it lands.
