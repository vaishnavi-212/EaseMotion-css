# Chladni Plate

A pure CSS/HTML Chladni plate: a bow drawn across the edge, and sand collecting into a different figure for every mode.

## What it shows

Ernst Chladni demonstrated this in 1787 and it is still the clearest picture of a standing wave anyone has made.

Sprinkle sand on a metal plate clamped at its centre, draw a violin bow across the free edge, and the plate rings. Most of it is moving, and sand there gets thrown off. But a standing wave has **nodal lines**, places that do not move at all, and the sand skitters around until it falls onto one and stays. The pattern you see is not a picture of the vibration. It is a picture of the parts of the plate that are **not** vibrating.

Change the note and you get a completely different figure, because a different mode has different nodes. Chladni toured Europe doing this, and it later gave Sophie Germain and others the problem that became plate theory.

## How it is built

- **The grains are solved for, not drawn.** Every grain sits at a solution of the square-plate mode equation

  `cos(m·π·x)·cos(n·π·y) − cos(n·π·x)·cos(m·π·y) = 0`

  for its own `(m,n)`. The positions were generated by sampling that function and keeping points whose distance to the zero set, `|v| / |∇v|`, is under a threshold. Normalising by the gradient matters: without it the flat regions of one mode produced a smear instead of a line.

- **Verified independently in the browser.** The check recomputes the mode equation from scratch against each grain's own declared `--x`, `--y`, `--m` and measures its distance to the nodal line. All **425 grains** across four modes come out within **0.0109**. Nothing is on a line by accident, and none of it was placed by eye.

- **Four real modes**: (1,2), (1,3), (2,3) and (1,4), each holding for a quarter of the cycle. Switching mode does not move any grain. The grains for the other three modes simply are not there, which is what changing the note actually does.

- **The plate barely moves**: the hum is `scale(1.004)` and a tenth of a degree, because a ringing plate's amplitude is tiny. The drama is in the sand, not the plate.

No images, no JavaScript, no external assets.

## Accessibility

`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` disables every keyframe and leaves the first mode's figure standing with its label. The figure is the content, so it survives with motion off.
